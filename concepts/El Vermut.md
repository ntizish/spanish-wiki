# El Vermut

**Domain:** Food & social culture · **Period:** 19th century – present

The Sunday aperitivo ritual. Going for "el vermut" (or "el vermú" in Spanish) means meeting friends or family around noon on Sunday, ordering vermouth and small snacks, and lingering for an hour or two before lunch. It is less about the drink than the ritual.

## What It Is

Vermouth — a fortified, aromatized wine — arrived in Catalonia from Turin in the early 19th century. Barcelona's port neighborhood (Barceloneta) and the working-class districts were the first to embrace it. By the mid-20th century, the Sunday vermut stop between mass and the family meal was embedded in Catalan and Spanish social life.

The drink itself: usually a **Vermut Rojo** (sweet red vermouth), served over ice with an orange slice and an olive. Some bars serve it on draft (*de grifo*). A splash of soda is common. A *Blanco* (white, slightly drier) or *Rosso* is also ordered. The glass is always small — this is a prelude, not the main event.

## The Spread

A full vermut at a good bar comes with:
- **Olives** — usually manzanilla, often with a toothpick
- **Patatas bravas** or chips
- **Anchovies** (*anxoves*) — particularly Catalan; the L'Escala anchovies from the Costa Brava are famous
- **Boquerones en vinagre** — white anchovies in vinegar
- **Conserves** — tinned seafood (mussels, clams, razor clams) eaten straight from the tin; a Catalan bar tradition
- **Pan con tomate** (*pa amb tomàquet*) — bread rubbed with tomato and olive oil; the baseline of Catalan eating

## Where in Barcelona

The vermut culture is particularly strong in certain neighborhoods:

| Neighborhood | Character |
|---|---|
| **Gràcia** | Dense with old-school bars; the Plaça del Sol and Plaça de la Vila de Gràcia are classic spots |
| **Sant Antoni** | The Mercat de Sant Antoni renovation brought a wave of good vermut bars; very popular now |
| **El Born** | Touristy but still good; Bar del Pla, El Xampanyet |
| **Barceloneta** | The port origin; La Cova Fumada (birthplace of the bombas) is nearby |
| **Poble Sec** | Carrer de Blai (pintxos street) and surrounding bars |

## The Timing

The window is roughly **12:00 – 14:30** on Sundays (and increasingly Saturdays). By 14:30 people move to lunch. The whole sequence — vermut, lunch, sobremesa — can absorb the entire afternoon until 5 or 6pm. This is not a schedule failure; it is the plan.

## Catalan Vermut Brands

- **Yzaguirre** — produced in Bot (Terra Alta, Catalonia); one of the most respected Spanish vermuts
- **Miró** — also Catalan, from Reus (Tarragona)
- **Lustau** — technically Andalusian but widely drunk; their Rojo is a bar standard
- **Martini Rojo** — the Italian standard; ubiquitous and unpretentious

## Notes

The vermut revival — it had gone somewhat unfashionable by the 1990s — is real and now complete. It was driven partly by young Barcelonins reclaiming their grandparents' ritual, partly by neighborhood bars leaning into their heritage. It is now thoroughly fashionable without having lost its original character.
