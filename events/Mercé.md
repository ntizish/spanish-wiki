# La Mercè

**Type:** City festival · **Date:** September 24 (week of events from ~Sept 19) · **Region:** Barcelona

Barcelona's main annual festival, celebrating the city's co-patron: La Verge de la Mercè (Our Lady of Mercy). Five days of free concerts, human towers, fire runs, giant figures, and street parties across the city. If you're in Barcelona in late September, you'll be in it whether you planned to be or not.

## The Patron Saint

La Verge de la Mercè became co-patron of Barcelona in 1687, when a plague of locusts devastated the region. The city's authorities vowed to make her the patron if the plague ended; it did. Her feast day is September 24. The Basílica de la Mercè in the Gothic Quarter is her main church.

(Barcelona has two co-patrons: La Mercè and [[people/Sant Jordi|Sant Jordi]]. La Mercè gets the street party; Sant Jordi gets the books and roses.)

## The Events

La Mercè is coordinated by the Barcelona city council (Ajuntament). Almost all events are free.

### Concerts (BAM — Barcelona Acció Musical)
The musical program runs across multiple outdoor stages simultaneously — Parc de la Ciutadella, Passeig de Gràcia, Plaça de la Barceloneta, Montjuïc, and dozens of smaller venues. The lineup mixes international acts with Catalan and Spanish artists. Genuinely good programming; comparable caliber to paid festivals.

### Correfoc
The fire run. The most visceral event of the festival.

Groups (colles de diables — "devil gangs") dressed as demons and monsters parade through the streets wielding spinning fireworks, shooting sparks into the crowd. Participants enter the parade wearing old clothes, scarves over their mouths, and (optionally) goggles. The sparks land on you. This is expected and part of the point.

The tradition has roots in medieval religious processions (the *Ball de Diables*), and in Catalonia it is ancient and widespread — La Mercè's Correfoc is the largest. There is a children's Correfoc (Correfoc Infantil) earlier in the week for families.

### Castellers (Human Towers)
Several of Catalonia's main *colles castelleres* (human tower groups) converge on the Plaça de Sant Jaume (in front of the Ajuntament and the Palau de la Generalitat) to build their towers. This is one of the most important castellera events of the year.

Human towers — *castells* — are a Catalan tradition dating to the 18th century, declared UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage in 2010. The structure:
- **Pinya** — the wide base; dozens of people packed tight absorbing the weight
- **Tronc** — the tower's body, 4–10+ people high
- **Pom de dalt** — the top levels; small, light adults and children
- **Enxaneta** — the child at the very top who raises four fingers (representing the four red stripes of the Catalan flag) to signal completion

The measurement: the number of people per level × the number of levels. A *3 de 10* (three people per level, ten levels high) is among the hardest; only a few colles can build it.

### Gegants (Giants)
The *gegants* (giants) — large papier-mâché or fibreglass figures carried by people inside them — parade through the Gothic Quarter. Barcelona has dozens of official gegants, each with names and histories. The gegants dance. It is slower and more ceremonial than the Correfoc; more family-oriented.

### Havaneres at Barceloneta
A concert of *havaneres* — Cuban-influenced sea shanties brought back to Catalonia by sailors in the 19th century — on the beach at Barceloneta. The traditional drink is *cremat*: rum flambéed with coffee, cinnamon, and lemon peel. The combination of the music, the beach, the fire, and the cremat is atmospheric.

### Piromusical
La Mercè closes on September 24 with a **Piromusical** at the Font Màgica (Magic Fountain) on Montjuïc: a synchronized fireworks display set to music. Enormous crowd; arrive early for a good position.

## Practical Notes

- The week is genuinely crowded. Barcelona is always full in September (still high season), and La Mercè brings extra visitors
- Most events are outdoors and free; check the Ajuntament's program each year for specifics
- For the Correfoc: wear clothes you don't care about, cover your hair, bring a scarf for your face. Goggles are optional but some people use them
- For Castellers at Plaça Sant Jaume: arrive early; the square fills completely
- Accommodation prices peak this week

## Notes

The Correfoc is the thing most worth experiencing if you've never done it. There is nothing else quite like walking into a street full of people shooting fireworks at you while dressed as the devil. Go in the Correfoc Infantil if you want to understand the scale before committing.
