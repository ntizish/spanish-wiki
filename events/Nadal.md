# Nadal (Christmas)

**Type:** Religious & cultural holiday · **Period:** Dec 24–25 (season Dec 8 – Jan 6) · **Region:** All Spain

Christmas in Catalonia has the same core as everywhere in the Christian world — and then several traditions that exist nowhere else on earth.

## Key Dates in the Season

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| December 8 | Immaculate Conception (public holiday); Tió de Nadal begins being "fed" |
| December 22 | El Gordo lottery draw (Spain's massive Christmas lottery) |
| December 24 | Nit de Nadal (Christmas Eve); main family dinner |
| December 25 | Nadal (Christmas Day); gifts from Pare Noel in some families |
| December 26 | Sant Esteve — a second Christmas feast, distinctly Catalan; canelons are traditional |
| January 5 | Cavalcada de Reis (Kings parade) |
| January 6 | [[events/Tres Reyes Magos|Els Reis Mags]] — Epiphany; children receive gifts |

## Uniquely Catalan Traditions

### Tió de Nadal (El Cagatio)

The Tió is a hollow log with a painted face, little legs, and a blanket. Starting December 8, the family "feeds" it every night (nuts, fruit, sweets). On Christmas Eve or Christmas Day, children beat the log with sticks while singing:

> *Caga tió, caga torró, avellanes i mató, si no cagues bé, et donaré un cop de bastó!*
> ("Poop, log, poop nougat, hazelnuts and fresh cheese, if you don't poop well, I'll hit you with a stick!")

The blanket is then lifted to reveal the gifts the log has "defecated" — traditionally sweets and small presents, with the poop metaphor maintained (turró, mandarines, etc.). A salt herring or piece of garlic signals the log is empty.

The Tió is a pre-Christian fertility symbol, the warmth of the hearth personified, now joyfully domesticated into a children's ritual.

### El Caganer

Hidden in every traditional Catalan nativity scene (pessebre) is [[people/El Caganer|El Caganer]] — a figure of a peasant squatting with trousers down, defecating. The positioning of the figure in the corner of the scene is a game for children. See the dedicated page.

### El Pessebre (Nativity Scene)

The nativity scene (pessebre, from the word for manger) is taken very seriously in Catalonia. Elaborate, large-scale pessebres are displayed publicly in churches and town halls. The Pessebre Vivent (Living Nativity) is performed in many towns.

## Shared Spanish Traditions

- **El Gordo** — Spain's Christmas lottery on December 22; one of the world's largest; watching the draw is a national ritual
- **Turró** — nougat in various forms; Catalan turró de Xixona (soft) and turró dur (hard almond) are the classics
- **Panettone** — imported Italian tradition, now ubiquitous
- **Cava** — Catalonia's sparkling wine is the standard toast

## Food

- **Escudella i carn d'olla** — Christmas Day's traditional Catalan stew; a two-course meal from one pot (broth first, then meat and vegetables)
- **Canelons de Sant Esteve** — on December 26, leftover Christmas meats are used to fill caneloni pasta; a uniquely Catalan tradition
- **Turró** (all forms)
- **Neules** — thin rolled wafer cookies hung on Christmas trees or dipped in cava

## Notes

Sant Esteve (December 26) is a public holiday in Catalonia but not in the rest of Spain — one of the small markers of Catalan distinctiveness. The canelons tradition on that day is firmly embedded; most Catalan families eat them.
