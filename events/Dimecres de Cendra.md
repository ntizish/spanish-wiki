# Dimecres de Cendra (Ash Wednesday)

**Type:** Religious observance · **Date:** Variable (day after Fat Tuesday) · **Region:** All Spain

The first day of Lent. A Catholic priest marks the forehead of the faithful with ash in the shape of a cross, saying *"Remember that you are dust, and to dust you shall return."* Forty days of fasting and abstinence begin.

## Origins

The imposition of ashes as a sign of penance appears in the Old Testament. The practice became a formal liturgical rite in the Western Church around the 10th–11th centuries. The ashes traditionally come from burning the palm branches blessed on the previous year's Palm Sunday.

## The Forty Days

The period from Ash Wednesday to Holy Saturday is 40 days (Sundays are excluded from the count as mini-Easters). The number echoes:
- Jesus's 40 days in the desert
- Moses's 40 years in the desert
- Noah's 40 days of rain

Traditional Lenten observances:
- No meat on Fridays (fish and bacallà instead)
- Fasting or abstinence on Ash Wednesday and Good Friday
- In Catalonia: **bacallà a la llauna**, **escudella**, and other simple dishes become prominent

## In Catalonia: L'Enterrament de la Sardina

On Ash Wednesday, Carnaval officially ends with the symbolic **burial of the sardine** (Enterrament de la Sardina). A sardine (or a sardine effigy) is ceremonially buried or burned, marking the end of excess and the start of austerity. The mood is mock-funereal and comic.

This tradition exists across Spain but is celebrated with particular theatricality in Catalonia and Madrid.

## Notes

Ash Wednesday is not a public holiday in Spain or Catalonia — people go to work and then to mass. The ash cross on foreheads is still commonly seen on the street in Spain in a way that might surprise people from more secular countries.
