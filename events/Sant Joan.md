# Sant Joan (La Nit del Foc)

**Type:** Festival · **Date:** Night of June 23 / June 24 · **Region:** Catalonia, all Spain

Midsummer. The night of June 23 is the most spectacular night of the Catalan calendar — bonfires, fireworks (both professional and amateur, everywhere and all night), the coca de Sant Joan, and staying out until dawn. June 24 is the feast day of Saint John the Baptist; the eve is when everything happens.

## What Actually Happens

- **Fireworks** — more fireworks per capita than anywhere in Europe, seemingly. Both organized displays and individual families and neighbors setting off their own throughout the night. It is very loud.
- **Bonfires** — lit in town squares, on beaches, in neighborhoods. The tradition is to jump over the bonfire three times for luck and to purify yourself for the year ahead.
- **Coca de Sant Joan** — the essential food of the evening (see below)
- **Cava** — consumed in large quantities
- The night is essentially a city-wide street party; people move between bonfires, watch fireworks from beaches and rooftops, and stay out until sunrise

In Barcelona, the beach (Barceloneta, Bogatell) is the main gathering point. Expect enormous crowds.

## Origins

The midsummer fire festival is pre-Christian — fire rituals around the summer solstice exist across Europe. The Church absorbed the date as the feast of John the Baptist (who was born, per Luke, six months before Jesus — so six months before December 25 = June 24). The fire traditions were retained and Christianized.

## The Flama del Canigó

One of Catalonia's most powerful symbols. The **Flama del Canigó** (Flame of Canigó) is a torch lit each June 23 at the summit of Mount Canigó (in the Pyrenees, in French Catalonia / Catalunya Nord), then carried by runners to light bonfires across Catalonia and Catalan-speaking lands.

The tradition began in 1955 during the Franco dictatorship, when Catalan culture was suppressed — it was an act of cultural resistance. Today it lights thousands of bonfires across Catalonia, Valencia, the Balearics, and Roussillon (France).

## Coca de Sant Joan

A flat, sweet pastry — the defining food of the holiday. Varieties:
- **Coca de crema** — topped with pastry cream and candied fruit (the classic)
- **Coca de llardons** — with pork crackling (a savory-sweet combination)
- **Coca de fruites** — candied fruit throughout

Bakeries sell them from the afternoon of June 23; families buy one (or two) for the evening.

## Notes

Sant Joan is one of those nights where Barcelona empties of its usual self and becomes genuinely strange and festive. Earplugs are useful if you want to sleep. The amateur fireworks are illegal but universally tolerated; hospitals do see a spike in hand injuries.
