# Semana Santa

**Type:** Religious holiday · **Period:** Palm Sunday → Easter Sunday · **Region:** All Spain

Holy Week: the most solemn week in the Catholic calendar, commemorating the Passion, death, and resurrection of Jesus Christ. In Andalusia it's a spectacle of baroque devotion; in Catalonia it's quieter and more domestic — though processions do exist.

## Origins

Rooted in early Christian liturgy. The processional tradition as Spaniards know it crystallized in the 16th–17th centuries, fueled by Counter-Reformation devotional orders (cofradías/hermandades) who competed to build ever more elaborate floats (pasos).

## In Catalonia

Much less dramatic than Sevilla or Málaga. The main Catalan traditions are:
- **Mona de Pasqua** — an elaborate chocolate sculpture given by godparents to godchildren on Easter Sunday
- **Processions** — exist but subdued; Verges (Girona) has a famous one with dancing skeletons (Dansa de la Mort)
- Schools and many businesses close for the full week

## Contrast: Andalusia

The Semana Santa of Sevilla and Málaga is a different world — hundreds of cofradías, pasos weighing tons, nazarenos in pointed capirotes, saetas sung spontaneously from balconies.

## Food

- **Torrijas** — bread soaked in milk/wine, fried, sugared (ubiquitous across Spain)
- **Bacallà** (salt cod) — Fridays in Lent are meatless; cod dishes are everywhere in Catalonia
- **Bunyols** — fried dough, especially in Catalonia

## Notes

The pointed capirote hoods worn by nazarenos are frequently misread by non-Spaniards. They pre-date and are entirely unrelated to the KKK — penitential dress centuries older.
