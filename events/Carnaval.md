# Carnaval (Carnestoltes)

**Type:** Festival · **Period:** Variable (week before Ash Wednesday) · **Region:** All Spain

The pre-Lent explosion of excess before 40 days of fasting and austerity. Costumes, parades, music, and a general suspension of normal social rules. The name derives from Latin *carne vale* — "farewell to meat."

## Origins

Pagan midwinter festivals absorbed into the Christian calendar as the last blowout before Lent. The timing is entirely determined by Easter, which itself follows the lunar calendar — so Carnaval floats between early February and early March.

## In Catalonia

Catalonia's carnaval is called **Carnestoltes**. Key features:

- **El Rei Carnestoltes** — a carnival "king" figure who reigns for the week, personifying excess and disorder
- **L'Enterrament de la Sardina** — the burial of the sardine on Ash Wednesday, symbolically ending the festivities
- Costume parades (Rua de Carnaval) in every town

### Notable Carnavals in Catalonia

| Location | Character |
|----------|-----------|
| **Sitges** | The most famous in Catalonia; known for its elaborate floats and LGBTQ+ celebration; draws crowds from Barcelona |
| **Vilanova i la Geltrú** | Famous for the *batalla de caramels* (candy battle) and its own distinct traditions going back centuries |
| **Solsona** | Renowned for a giant carnival figure and fireworks |
| **Barcelona** | Big Rua parade in Gràcia and Sant Andreu neighborhoods |

## Key Moments

- **Dijous Gras (Fat Thursday)** — the carnaval begins; traditional foods like botifarra and bunyols
- **Dimarts de Carnaval (Fat Tuesday / Mardi Gras)** — the peak night
- **Dimecres de Cendra** — [[events/Dimecres de Cendra|Ash Wednesday]] ends it all

## Food

- **Bunyols de Carnaval** — fried dough, sweet, eaten throughout the season
- **Botifarra d'ou** — egg sausage, a Carnaval-specific product in Catalonia
- **Coca de llardons** — rich flatbread with pork crackling

## Notes

Franco banned Carnaval during the dictatorship (1939–1975) as immoral and disorderly. Many towns lost decades of tradition. The revival after the Transición was enthusiastic — Sitges in particular rebuilt its carnival into one of the most celebrated in Spain. See [[people/Francisco Franco|Francisco Franco]].
