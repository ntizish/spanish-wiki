# Sant Jordi

**Type:** Cultural holiday · **Date:** April 23 · **Region:** Catalonia (especially)

Catalonia's most beloved secular-ish holiday. Also UNESCO World Book Day. The streets fill with stalls selling roses and books; couples and friends exchange them. If you're in Barcelona on April 23, go out — La Rambla and the Gothic Quarter are extraordinary.

## The Tradition

- **Men give women roses** — traditionally
- **Women give men books** — traditionally
- Now: everyone gives anyone what they like; both roses and books are common gifts in all directions

By the end of the day, millions of roses and hundreds of thousands of books are sold across Catalonia. It's the biggest book-selling day of the year.

## Origins

Two traditions merged on April 23:

1. **Feast of Sant Jordi** (Saint George) — patron saint of Catalonia. The rose tradition is linked to the legend: where the dragon's blood fell, a rosebush grew.
2. **World Book Day** — UNESCO chose April 23 in 1995 partly because it's the date of death of both Cervantes and Shakespeare (April 23, 1616).

The book-giving tradition in Catalonia actually predates UNESCO's involvement — it began in 1930.

## The Legend

[[people/Sant Jordi|Sant Jordi]] slew a dragon that was terrorizing a town (some versions: the kingdom of Montblanc, in Catalonia). He rescued the princess. Where the dragon's blood soaked the ground, a red rose grew. Sant Jordi gave it to the princess.

## The Day in Practice

- Bookstalls and flower stalls cover every major street
- Publishers release their most anticipated books of the year for this date
- Authors sit at tables signing copies all day
- Libraries and schools organize readings
- Many Catalan institutions (the Generalitat, the Palau de la Generalitat) open to the public

## Notes

Sant Jordi is also patron saint of England, Georgia, and several other places — but nowhere celebrates it quite like Catalonia. On rainy April 23s, the mood is still festive; people just carry umbrellas with their roses.
