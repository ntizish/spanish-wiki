# Tres Reyes Magos (Els Reis Mags)

**Type:** Religious holiday · **Date:** January 6 (Epiphany) · **Region:** All Spain

The Three Kings — Melchior, Caspar, and Balthasar — arrive on the night of January 5 to bring gifts to children. Traditionally more important than Christmas Day itself for gift-giving, though Santa Claus has been gaining ground in Catalonia.

## Origins

Epiphany commemorates the Magi's visit to the infant Jesus, as told in Matthew 2:1–12. The gift-giving tradition developed in Spain from the 19th century and became the dominant children's holiday by the 20th.

## The Cavalcada de Reis (January 5 evening)

The parade is the main event. In every town, the Three Kings arrive by boat, horse, helicopter, or train (Barcelona has a spectacular arrival by boat at the port), then parade through the streets throwing sweets (caramels) to the crowd.

- **Barcelona's cavalcada** is one of the largest in Spain — a major evening spectacle
- Children line the streets to catch candy and catch a glimpse of the kings
- It's genuinely cold in January; dress for it

## The Night of January 5

- Children leave their shoes out (not stockings) for the Kings to fill with gifts
- A glass of water and something to eat is left for the camels
- Good children get presents; naughty ones traditionally get **carbó** (coal) — now sold as sugar coal candy

## Roscón de Reyes (Tortell de Reis in Catalan)

A ring-shaped sweet bread decorated with candied fruit, eaten on January 6. Hidden inside:
- A small figurine (who finds it is "king for the day")
- A dried bean (who finds it has to pay for the next one)

## Notes

Increasingly, Catalan families celebrate both Santa Claus (December 25) and the Kings (January 6), doubling the gift occasions. Purists lament this; children do not.
