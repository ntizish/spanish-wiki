# Sant Jordi (Saint George)

**~275 – ~303 AD** · Cappadocia · Roman soldier, Christian martyr, patron saint of Catalonia

A Roman soldier from Cappadocia (modern Turkey) who became a Christian martyr under Emperor Diocletian. Historically a minor figure; legendarily enormous. Patron saint of Catalonia, England, Georgia, Ethiopia, Portugal, and dozens of other places — but nowhere is his feast day celebrated quite like [[events/Sant Jordi|April 23 in Catalonia]].

## Historical Sant Jordi

Very little is historically certain about the real George. What is generally accepted:
- He was a soldier in the Roman army, likely from Cappadocia
- He was a Christian who refused to renounce his faith during Diocletian's persecutions
- He was executed around 303 AD in Lydda (now Lod, Israel)
- He was venerated as a martyr very early in the Eastern Church

The dragon — not historical. The princess — not historical. The dragon story attaches to him centuries after his death.

## The Dragon Legend

The legend as told in Catalonia:

A dragon had taken up residence near the town of **Montblanc** (in Catalonia, in some versions; the Holy Land in others) and was terrorizing the population. The townspeople fed it sheep, then, when the sheep ran out, people — chosen by lottery. One day the lottery fell on the king's daughter (the princess).

As she was led out to the dragon, **Sant Jordi** appeared on a white horse. He slew the dragon with his lance. Where the dragon's blood fell on the ground, a rosebush grew. Sant Jordi gave the first rose to the princess.

This story is first attached to George in the 11th–12th centuries, during the Crusades, when George became a warrior-patron for Crusader armies. It appears in *The Golden Legend* (Legenda Aurea), compiled by Jacobus de Voragine around 1260.

## Why Catalonia?

Aragonese-Catalan crusaders and soldiers adopted Sant Jordi as their patron in the 12th–13th centuries. The Crown of Aragon carried his red cross on a white field — the same symbol now on the Catalan flag's diagonal stripes (the *Senyera* predates this, but the association deepened). The **Generalitat de Catalunya** is headquartered in the **Palau de la Generalitat**, whose chapel is dedicated to Sant Jordi.

## April 23

April 23 is the date of George's martyrdom (disputed historically, but traditional). UNESCO designated it World Book Day in 1995, partly because Cervantes and Shakespeare both died on April 23, 1616. In Catalonia, the book-and-rose tradition on this day is older than UNESCO's involvement — books were added to the rose tradition in 1930. See [[events/Sant Jordi|Sant Jordi (event)]].

## The Red Cross of Sant Jordi

The **Creu de Sant Jordi** (red cross on white ground) appears throughout Catalan public life:
- On the facade of the Palau de la Generalitat
- On the flag of England (the same cross — George is England's patron too)
- On heraldic shields throughout the Crown of Aragon's historical territories

## Notes

The overlap of Sant Jordi's patronage across Catalonia, England, and Georgia is a frequent source of mild amazement. All three adopted him through medieval military-religious devotion, independently. He appears on the flags of all three.
