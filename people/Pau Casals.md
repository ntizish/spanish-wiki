# Pau Casals (Pablo Casals)

**1876 – 1973** · El Vendrell, Catalonia · Cellist, conductor, Catalan exile

Universally considered the greatest cellist of the 20th century, and arguably ever. Beyond his music, Casals became a symbol of moral resistance — he lived in exile for 38 years, refusing to perform in any country that recognized [[people/Francisco Franco|Franco's]] regime. He outlived Franco by two years but chose to die without returning to Catalonia.

## Who He Was

Born in El Vendrell (Tarragona) in 1876, son of a church organist. A prodigy — playing cello at 12, internationally known at 22. His transformative act as a musician: in his early 20s he discovered Bach's Six Cello Suites in a Barcelona secondhand shop (his father brought him there). These suites had been largely ignored for 170 years. Casals studied them for 12 years before performing them publicly. His recordings of the Suites (1936–39) are foundational documents of classical music.

## Musical Legacy

- **Bach Cello Suites** — his recordings and performances restored these works to the center of the repertoire
- **Cello technique** — transformed how the instrument was played; his bowing arm technique and expressive range were new
- **Prades Festival** — founded in Prades (French Catalonia) in 1950; brought music to his exile village and remains active
- **Orquestra Pau Casals** — he founded and conducted this Barcelona orchestra before the Civil War; it was dissolved under Franco

## The Exile

When Franco's forces entered Barcelona in January 1939, Casals crossed the Pyrenees on foot with thousands of Republican refugees. He settled in **Prades** (French Catalonia / Catalunya Nord), a Catalan-speaking village in the French Pyrenees.

His vow: he would not perform publicly in any country that recognized Franco's Spain. This meant:

- No concerts in Western Europe (which recognized Franco)
- No concerts in the US (which also recognized Franco, strategically, during the Cold War)
- A decade of near-silence — he played privately, for himself, but the world couldn't hear him

## The Return to the Stage

In 1950, musicians including violinist Alexander Schneider persuaded Casals to perform at a festival in Prades to mark the bicentenary of Bach's death. Casals agreed — for Bach. The **Prades Festival** became annual. He resumed recording.

In 1961, he performed at the **White House** for President Kennedy — a contradiction of his vow, somewhat — but argued the Kennedy administration was different.

## The United Nations Address (1971)

At 95, Casals addressed the United Nations. He said:

> *"I am a Catalan. Catalonia had the first parliament long before England had one. Catalonia had the first United Nations. I say this from the depth of my heart."*

He then conducted his composition **El Pessebre** (The Manger) — an oratorio about the nativity, deeply connected to Catalan tradition.

## El Cant dels Ocells

The piece most associated with Casals in his later life. A Catalan folk song — *El Cant dels Ocells* (The Song of the Birds) — which he played as an encore at every concert from his exile years onward. He said he played it in memory of his homeland. It became a symbol of Catalan longing and resistance.

## Later Life and Death

He spent his final years in **Puerto Rico** (where his mother was from) with his young wife Marta Montañez. He died in San Juan in October 1973, two years before Franco's death. He never returned to Catalonia.

His remains were eventually brought back to El Vendrell in 1979, after Franco's death and the restoration of Catalan autonomy.
