# Mercè Rodoreda

**1908 – 1983** · Barcelona · Novelist, short story writer, Catalan literature's defining voice

The most important Catalan writer of the 20th century, and one of the most important novelists in any language from that period. She spent nearly 30 years in exile, wrote in a language that was banned, and produced work of extraordinary psychological depth. *La plaça del Diamant* is the novel to read.

## Who She Was

Born in Sant Gervasi (then a separate municipality, now part of Barcelona). Largely self-educated — she read obsessively. Published her first novels in the 1930s during the brief flowering of Catalan culture under the Republic. When Franco's forces took Barcelona in January 1939, she fled across the Pyrenees with the tide of Republican exiles. She was 30.

She would not return to Catalonia for nearly four decades.

## The Exile Years

Rodoreda spent the war years in France, then moved to Geneva in 1954, where she worked for the Institut d'Estudis Catalans (the Catalan cultural institution in exile) and lived for over 20 years.

This is when she wrote her best work — in Catalan, a language banned in Spain, for an audience that could barely access her books. She was effectively writing into a void. The novels circulated in exile circles and in clandestine copies inside Catalonia.

She returned to Catalonia in 1972, settling in Romanyà de la Selva (a small village in the Baix Empordà), where she lived until her death in 1983.

## La Plaça del Diamant (1962)

Her masterpiece. Published in exile by Club dels Novel·listes (Barcelona). The novel tells the story of **Natàlia** (nicknamed "Colometa" — little dove) from the early 1930s through the Civil War and its aftermath. The narration is entirely in her first-person voice — intimate, digressive, apparently simple, actually precise.

The *plaça del Diamant* is a real square in Gràcia, Barcelona. It still exists. People leave flowers there.

What makes the novel extraordinary:
- The narration captures how an ordinary person processes extraordinary events without fully understanding them — the Civil War enters the novel sideways, through disruption and absence
- The prose style mirrors Natàlia's consciousness: repetition, circularity, an inability to name certain things directly
- It is one of the few literary accounts of the Civil War written from the perspective of a working-class woman — not a soldier, not an intellectual, not a politician

It has been translated into more than 40 languages. The English translation (*The Time of the Doves*) is by David Rosenthal.

## Other Key Works

- **El carrer de les Camèlies** (1966) — another exile novel; a woman reconstructing her identity from fragmented memory
- **Jardí vora el mar** (1967) — shorter, more fable-like
- **La meva Cristina i altres contes** (1967) — short stories; dark, surreal, fairy-tale inflected; excellent
- **Mirall trencat** (1974) — a more ambitious, multigenerational family novel; written after her return
- **Quanta, quanta guerra…** (1980) — her final novel; more experimental

## Style and Themes

Rodoreda's recurring territory:
- **Women's interior lives** — especially working-class or lower-middle-class women constrained by circumstance and expectation
- **Memory and loss** — the past that cannot be recovered, the life that could have been different
- **The Civil War as background devastation** — never center stage, always reshaping everything
- **Nature and the body** — flowers appear constantly in her work, both beautiful and slightly threatening; her prose has a physical, sensory quality

## Connection to Barcelona

Her Barcelona is Gràcia — the village-neighborhood absorbed by the city. The Plaça del Diamant, the Mercat de l'Abaceria, the narrow streets. It is a working-class Barcelona of the 1930s that no longer exists physically but is preserved in her novels.

## Notes

If you read one book from this wiki, read *La plaça del Diamant*. It is short (~200 pages), widely available in translation, and unlike almost anything else. The Catalan original is considered one of the finest prose works in the language — if your Catalan is good enough, it's worth the original.
