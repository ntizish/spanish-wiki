# Francisco Franco

**1892 – 1975** · Galicia · Military general, head of state 1939–1975

The defining figure of 20th-century Spain. Franco ruled for 36 years through a combination of repression, strategic ambiguity, and slow economic modernization. Understanding modern Spain — its regional tensions, its relationship to memory, its politics — requires understanding Franco.

## Who He Was

Born in El Ferrol (Galicia) to a naval family. Rose through the military in the brutal colonial wars in Morocco, where he developed the ruthless tactics he would later use in Spain. Became the youngest general in Europe. In July 1936, he joined and quickly led the military uprising against the elected Republican government — beginning the Spanish Civil War.

## The Civil War (1936–1939)

The war lasted nearly three years. Franco's forces (Nationalists) received crucial support from Nazi Germany (air power, the Condor Legion — responsible for the bombing of Guernica) and Fascist Italy. The Republic was supported by the Soviet Union and the International Brigades (foreign volunteers).

Key moments:
- **Bombing of Guernica (1937)** — Basque town destroyed by the Condor Legion; immortalized by Picasso's painting
- **Battle of the Ebro (1938)** — the war's largest battle, fought partly in Catalonia; the Republic's last major offensive
- **Fall of Barcelona (January 1939)** — mass exodus of Catalans and Republicans across the French border
- **Fall of Madrid (March 1939)** — end of the war; Franco declared victory

## The Dictatorship (1939–1975)

Franco's Spain was not a fixed system — it evolved across four decades, shaped by internal factions and external pressures.

### Repression
- An estimated 100,000–200,000 killed in post-war reprisals and executions
- 500,000+ exiled
- Political parties, trade unions, and opposition banned
- The **Law of Historical Memory** (2007) and its successor (2022) are still grappling with identifying mass graves

### Catalonia Specifically
Franco's treatment of Catalonia was particularly harsh because Catalonia had been the Republic's industrial and financial backbone and a stronghold of resistance:
- **Catalan language banned** in public life, education, publishing, and official contexts
- The Generalitat (Catalan self-government) dissolved
- [[events/La Diada|La Diada]] suppressed
- [[events/Carnaval|Carnaval]], [[events/Sant Joan|Sant Joan]], and other regional festivals restricted or banned
- Lluís Companys, the President of the Generalitat, was captured in France, handed to Spain, tortured, and executed in 1940 at Montjuïc castle

### Economic Evolution
- 1940s: autarky (economic self-sufficiency) → near-famine; a disaster
- Late 1950s: *Los Tecnócratas* (Opus Dei economists) open Spain to foreign investment; the **Spanish Miracle** — rapid industrialization through the 1960s
- Tourism grows massively; the Costa Brava and Costa del Sol fill with Northern Europeans

### Later Period
Franco aged, his regime calcified. He named Juan Carlos (grandson of Alfonso XIII) as his successor, apparently expecting continuity. He was wrong.

## Death and the Transición

Franco died on November 20, 1975, after a prolonged medical ordeal. King Juan Carlos I was crowned two days later and, contrary to expectations, oversaw the **Transición** — Spain's transition to democracy:
- 1976: Political Reform Act
- 1977: First free elections since 1936
- 1978: Spanish Constitution
- 1979: Statute of Autonomy for Catalonia and the Basque Country

The Transición is often held up as a model democratic transition but is also criticized for its *pacto de olvido* (pact of forgetting) — an implicit agreement not to prosecute Francoist crimes in exchange for peaceful transition.

## The Valley of the Fallen / Valle de los Caídos

Franco built a vast mausoleum near Madrid — the **Valle de los Caídos** — using Republican prisoner labor. He was buried there until 2019, when the Socialist government exhumed and reinterred his remains in a family mausoleum, amid political controversy.

## Legacy

Franco remains deeply divisive. The **Law of Democratic Memory (2022)** declared Francoism illegitimate, but his statue still stands in some places, and debate over his legacy continues in Spanish politics. In Catalonia, he is an uncomplicated villain; in other parts of Spain, the picture is murkier.
