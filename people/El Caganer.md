# El Caganer

**17th century – present** · Catalonia · Folk nativity figure

The Caganer (literally "the one who defecates" in Catalan) is a small figurine hidden in Catalan nativity scenes (pessebres): a peasant with his trousers around his ankles, squatting, in the act of defecating. He is tucked into a corner away from the holy family and the finding of him is a game for children.

He is one of the most distinctly Catalan things that exist.

## Origins

The earliest documented Caganers appear in 18th-century Catalan nativity scenes, though the tradition likely predates this. His origins are debated:

- **Fertility theory:** The act of defecating fertilizes the earth; placing him in the nativity scene ensures a good harvest for the coming year — a pre-Christian agricultural magic absorbed into Christmas tradition
- **Leveling theory:** He brings the sacred down to the earthly; the divine event of the nativity is grounded by the most human of acts
- **Simple folk humor:** Catalan culture has a robust tradition of scatological humor (see also: [[events/Nadal|Tió de Nadal]])

Attempts by the Catholic Church to remove him from nativity scenes in the 18th century were unsuccessful.

## The Traditional Figure

The classic Caganer is a peasant in traditional Catalan dress:
- Red *barretina* (stocking cap)
- White shirt
- Black trousers at ankles
- Bare bottom

## Celebrity Caganers

From the late 20th century, manufacturers began producing Caganers modeled on famous people. This has become a thriving industry in Barcelona's markets (especially the Fira de Santa Llúcia, the Christmas market outside the Cathedral). Every year brings new editions:
- Politicians (Barack Obama, Donald Trump, various Spanish PMs)
- Footballers (Messi is perennially popular)
- Royals (King Felipe VI, Queen Letizia)
- Cultural figures, Pope Francis

The political ones are particularly popular — there is something satisfying about a satirical image of a powerful figure in this position.

## Where to Find Him

- **Fira de Santa Llúcia** (Barcelona Cathedral, November–December) — the main Christmas market; dedicated Caganer stalls
- **El Rei de la Màgia** — traditional Catalan magic/toy shop in El Born, stocks them year-round
- Inside any traditional Catalan pessebre

## Cultural Significance

The Caganer encapsulates something real about Catalan folk culture: an earthiness, a refusal of pure solemnity, and an egalitarian humor that punctures pretension. Combined with the [[events/Nadal|Tió de Nadal]] (the defecating log), Catalan Christmas has a notably scatological character compared to Christmas anywhere else in Europe.

## Notes

Non-Catalans often assume the Caganer is a joke or a modern invention. He is neither — he's been in Catalan pessebres for at least 300 years and is taken seriously as a cultural inheritance. Parents teach children where to look for him.
